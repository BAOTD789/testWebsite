SnapSnack

Project Mission

  • Due to the pandemic, people were more interested in cooking at home. However, some had little to no experience or had no idea on how to start with the ingredients in their fridge.

  • With Snap snack, the mobile application goal was to show a multitude of recipes just by scanning an ingredient in your fridge. With Snap Snack, you would scan an ingredient in your fridge and it would provide a recipe that would pair along with a culmination of the ingredients you scanned.

Problem Statement

  • The start of our development towards this app was directed towards the problem statement and thinking about problems that Snapsnack can effectively tackle and the audience that would be interested in this problem solver. The main problem that our team thought a food-recognition app would solve is creating a meal from the ingredients left over. As well as the idea that this application is perfect for people who need to get that first push towards getting into home cooking. Lastly, the problem statement became wrapped up in a neat little bow with a nice catchy line. “If you need a Snack, do it in a Snap!”

  • Statement
    • You probably know the daunting feeling of opening your refrigerator only to be greeted with nothing but tons of ingredients left over from the meals you recently made. It can be hard to craft a cohesive and delicious meal with this amalgamation of resources. Using SnapSnack, all you need to do is take a picture of the contents of your fridge and/or pantry and you will be presented with recipes that utilize the ingredients you have. Being able to quickly find a recipe to use will help you reduce the amount of good food tossed out. This Home application is perfect for the hungry college student that doesn’t know what to cook and needs that burst of inspiration from the food they have in their pantry and fridge. With recipes formed by the ingredients that you scanned, you can make any situation into a fast meal. If you need a Snack, do it in a Snap!

Competitive Analysis

  • When formulating our Competitive Analysis, we needed to understand which features should take priority in a food-recognition recipe app. So my team and I looked online for competitors that had a similar concept to our idea of SnapSnack. Some features we took away were food recognition through the camera, keeping a history of old ingredients we scanned so you can recall old recipes, and having a vast recipe database to refer to. Though we couldn’t get all the features designed by the end of the quarter, this process helped guide our team in the right direction on thinking about the feature list for later.

Survey & Interviews

  • To gather more research on how SnapSnack should work and which type of people would be interested in a food-recognition recipe app; we turned to interviewing creators that made some of these apps we competitively researched. For learning more about how we would incorporate the food recognition system, we decided to schedule an interview with David Zeng about the work he had done for Calorie Mama. Unfortunately, he never reached back to us about his process in designing for Calorie Mama.

Personas

  • With having some difficulty deciding which students could help us out towards developing good demographic groups that would be interested in using an app like Snap Snack.

  • We were able to come up with five different potential groups:
    • exploratory home cooks
    • People that needed to adhere to dietary restrictions for various reasons (religious, allergy, physical fitness)
    • Health conscious people
    • general middle class population
    • college users
    • Persona I worked on: (College Users)

  • It worked out nicely that there were five members and five groups. Each one of the groups were able to condense the idea of the demographic to a proper persona.

Key Takeaways
